Dual Equilibrium Failure in Penalty Shootouts:
A Leverage-Based Test of Mixed-Strategy Nash Equilibrium Deviation

Adit Satpathy

Working paper · Data: StatsBomb Open Data (via statsbombpy) · July 2026

Abstract

Mixed-Strategy Nash Equilibrium (MSNE) predicts that penalty kickers and goalkeepers should randomize across shot/dive directions such that neither can improve their expected outcome by deviating — a prediction well supported by prior work on the corner shots of the game (Palacios-Huerta, 2003). This paper asks whether that equilibrium holds when the psychological stakes of a single kick are highest. Using 363 penalty kicks from 36 shootouts across 13 competitions in the StatsBomb open data corpus, we construct a leverage measure via Markov backward induction over the shootout state space and split kicks into low-leverage ("baseline") and high-leverage ("crisis") groups. We document a candidate dual equilibrium failure: under crisis conditions, kickers reduce center-shot usage by 25.6% relative to baseline (13.3% → 9.9%, consistent in direction but underpowered, $p=0.201$), while goalkeepers simultaneously abandon the mixed strategy in the opposite direction, becoming significantly more likely to stay center (3.4% → 9.7%, $p=0.024$, 95% CI excludes zero). Because the implied value of a center shot remains approximately 0.84 regardless of leverage, this pattern means kickers most avoid center exactly when it is most rewarding — a compounding inefficiency consistent with loss aversion in kickers and action bias in goalkeepers (Bar-Eli et al., 2007). A refinement analysis shows the kicker deviation is targeted: crisis kickers track the literature MSNE almost exactly on the Left/Right margin while specifically under-supplying Center. We report all results, including two null and underpowered tests, and discuss the sample-size and data-coverage limitations that bound the strength of these claims.

Keywords: game theory · mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium · behavioral economics · loss aversion · action bias · sports analytics · penalty kicks

1. Introduction

The penalty kick is one of the cleanest natural experiments available for testing game-theoretic equilibrium behavior. Two players — kicker and goalkeeper — choose actions simultaneously, payoffs are (approximately) common knowledge, the stage game repeats across thousands of professional matches, and outcomes are unambiguous. Palacios-Huerta (2003) used this setting to provide some of the most convincing field evidence to date that professional actors play in accordance with Mixed-Strategy Nash Equilibrium (MSNE): observed shot- and dive-direction frequencies track the equilibrium prediction closely, and the serial independence of consecutive choices is statistically indistinguishable from the random mixing MSNE requires.

That literature, however, treats every penalty kick as an identical draw from the same strategic environment. In practice, kicks vary enormously in psychological stakes: a first-round penalty in a shootout carries a different weight than a sudden-death kick that ends a World Cup final. If equilibrium play is a learned, effortful process — rather than an automatic reflex — then the moments where it is hardest to sustain are precisely the highest-leverage kicks, and these are the moments the existing literature's pooled, unconditional tests are least equipped to detect.

This paper introduces a within-shootout leverage measure, computed via Markov backward induction over the shootout state space, and uses it to split kicks into low-leverage ("baseline") and high-leverage ("crisis") sub-samples. We then test whether the MSNE prediction holds equally well in both regimes, focusing specifically on the Center zone of the goal — the zone whose use requires the kicker to bet against the goalkeeper's willingness to stay upright rather than dive, and whose neglect is the strategically distinctive symptom of pressure highlighted in the behavioral-economics literature on choking (Bar-Eli et al., 2007). Our central finding is a dual equilibrium failure: kickers reduce center-shot usage under crisis exactly as goalkeepers become more willing to stay and defend it, a compounding, mutually reinforcing deviation from equilibrium play driven by two distinct behavioral mechanisms operating on the two sides of the game simultaneously.

2. Theoretical Framework

2.1 The penalty kick as a zero-sum game

Following the standard treatment, model each penalty kick as a simultaneous-move, constant-sum game between a kicker and a goalkeeper. Both players choose among three zones of the goal, $\{L, C, R\}$ (Left, Center, Right). Let $\sigma_k = (p_L, p_C, p_R)$ denote the kicker's mixed strategy and $\sigma_g = (q_L, q_C, q_R)$ the goalkeeper's mixed strategy over the goalkeeper's own dive direction. Writing $\pi_{ij} = P(\text{goal} \mid \text{kick } i,\ \text{GK dives } j)$ for the empirical payoff matrix, the kicker's expected scoring probability is

$$ \Pi(\sigma_k, \sigma_g) \;=\; \sum_{i \in \{L,C,R\}} \sum_{j \in \{L,C,R\}} p_i\, q_j\, \pi_{ij}. $$

Because the game is (to a first approximation) zero-sum — the kicker wants to maximize $\Pi$, the goalkeeper wants to minimize it — the Nash equilibrium is a pair of mixed strategies $(\sigma_k^\ast, \sigma_g^\ast)$ satisfying the standard minimax indifference conditions: for every zone $i$ in the support of $\sigma_k^\ast$,

$$ \sum_{j} q_j^\ast\, \pi_{ij} \;=\; v \qquad \text{(the value of the game)}, $$

and symmetrically for the goalkeeper. Intuitively, at equilibrium a kicker must be indifferent between every zone they play with positive probability — otherwise they would shift more weight onto the higher-payoff zone, and the goalkeeper would in turn adjust their own mixing, until indifference across all three zones is restored. We estimate this payoff matrix directly from the 66 kicks in our sample with both kick direction and goalkeeper direction observed (Table 8, Figure 7); because that matrix is too sparse to yield a stable data-driven equilibrium (Section 3), our statistical tests instead benchmark observed play against the literature MSNE estimated by Palacios-Huerta (2003) on a much larger professional sample: $\sigma^{\text{lit}}_k = (0.415,\ 0.140,\ 0.445)$ for (Left, Center, Right).

2.2 Leverage via backward induction

To identify "pressure" without relying on subjective coding, we define the leverage of a given kick as the swing in the kicking team's win probability that the kick's outcome induces:

$$ \text{Leverage} \;=\; \big|\, W(\text{state} \mid \text{kick scores}) \;-\; W(\text{state} \mid \text{kick misses}) \,\big|, $$

where $W(\cdot)$ is the kicking team's probability of ultimately winning the shootout, computed by backward induction over the full shootout state space (kicks remaining for each side, current goal difference, whose turn it is), assuming a constant per-kick conversion probability $p = 0.72$ (the pooled professional rate reported in Palacios-Huerta, 2003) and five regulation rounds per side. This construction has an elegant closed form in sudden death: once regulation is exhausted and the shootout proceeds kick-pair by kick-pair, the kicking team's win probability $W$ satisfies the recursion

$$ W \;=\; p^2 W \;+\; p(1-p)\cdot 1 \;+\; (1-p)^2 W, $$

reflecting the three ways a sudden-death kick-pair can resolve: both score and the state repeats ($p^2$), the kicking team scores and the opponent misses so the kicking team wins immediately ($p(1-p)$), or both miss and the state repeats ($(1-p)^2$). Collecting terms,

$$ W\big[1 - p^2 - (1-p)^2\big] = p(1-p) \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; W \cdot 2p(1-p) = p(1-p) \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; W = \tfrac{1}{2}, $$

for any $p \in (0,1)$ — a clean proof that every sudden-death kick carries leverage exactly $0.5$, the maximum possible value, independent of the underlying conversion rate. Regulation-round leverage is generally lower and varies with the running score, giving continuous within-match variation that a simple "sudden death vs. not" indicator would discard. Across the full sample, leverage ranges from 0.020 to 0.500 with a median of 0.299 (Figure 1); we use this median to split the sample into a baseline group ($n=211$, leverage $\le 0.299$) and a crisis group ($n=152$, leverage $> 0.299$), with all 36 sudden-death kicks falling into the crisis group by construction.

2.3 The dual-failure hypothesis

We formalize three linked hypotheses, tested in turn in Section 4:

H1 — Kicker loss aversion. Center-shot usage falls under crisis: $P(\text{Center} \mid \text{crisis}) < P(\text{Center} \mid \text{baseline})$. A missed center shot risks a "Standing save" — a save requiring no committed dive — carrying a distinct psychological cost beyond its equal-leverage game-theoretic weight, which should be more aversive precisely when the kick matters most.

H2 — Goalkeeper action bias. The goalkeeper stay-center rate rises under crisis: $P(\text{GK stays} \mid \text{crisis}) > P(\text{GK stays} \mid \text{baseline})$. Under Bar-Eli et al.'s (2007) action-bias account, a goalkeeper who dives and is beaten looks less culpable than one who stands still and is beaten — but this bias should be dampened, not amplified, when the stakes (and thus the pull toward "doing something") are highest. Observing the goalkeeper nonetheless become more willing to stand still is the harder-to-explain, and hence more diagnostic, result.

H3 — Compounding (dual) failure. These two shifts interact: as goalkeepers become (modestly) more willing to defend the center, the expected value of a center shot for the kicker should rise or hold steady — yet kickers move in the opposite direction, abandoning center exactly as it becomes more rewarding. This is the paper's central and most novel claim.

3. Data & Methods

The sample consists of 363 penalty kicks drawn from 36 complete shootouts across 13 competitions, accessed via the StatsBomb open data corpus (statsbombpy v1.20.0): the African Cup of Nations 2023, multiple seasons of the Champions League, Copa America 2024, the FIFA World Cup (2018, 2022), UEFA Euro (2020, 2024), the UEFA Women's Euro (2022, 2025), the Women's World Cup (2019, 2023), the Copa del Rey, and the Indian Super League. This is the entirety of the shootout data StatsBomb makes publicly available at the time of writing.

Shot end-coordinates are mapped onto a 120×80 pitch grid (goal at $x=120$, posts at $y=36$ and $y=44$) and divided into three equal thirds of the 8-unit goal mouth, labelled Left, Center, and Right from the kicker's perspective; right-footed kickers' natural side is recoded following Palacios-Huerta (2003) for supplementary checks. The empirical split is 38.3% Left ($n=139$), 11.8% Center ($n=43$), and 49.9% Right ($n=181$), with a natural-side rate (excluding Center) of 63.8% — within the 55–65% range reported in the prior literature. Goalkeeper behavior is derived from StatsBomb's Goal Keeper event records (goalkeeper_technique, goalkeeper_body_part) rather than freeze-frame positional data, because freeze-frames record only the goalkeeper's starting (centered) position, not their dive; the binary gk_stayed indicator (1 = Standing, 0 = Diving) is available for 82.6% of kicks, while the specific dive direction is available for only 25.6%.

Leverage is computed as described in Section 2.2 and used to split the sample at its median (0.299) into baseline and crisis groups:

The 3-zone payoff matrix (Left/Center/Right for both kicker and goalkeeper) is built from the 66 kicks with both directions observed. Because 5 of its 9 combined cells — and all 9 cells in each of the baseline/crisis split matrices — contain fewer than 8 observations, a linear-programming solve for the data-driven MSNE (via SciPy's HiGHS solver) returns a degenerate corner solution (100% GK-Right) that we flag as unreliable rather than report as a finding. All hypothesis tests below therefore benchmark observed frequencies against the literature MSNE of Palacios-Huerta (2003), estimated on a much larger professional sample: $(p_L, p_C, p_R)^{\text{lit}} = (0.415,\ 0.140,\ 0.445)$. The single missing (Center, Center) cell — no observed kick where the kicker went center and the goalkeeper stayed center — is patched with Palacios-Huerta's literature estimate of 0.59 for $P(\text{goal}\mid\text{center},\text{GK stays})$ wherever the implied-value calculation in Section 4.5 requires it.

Statistical inference throughout uses two complementary approaches: (i) logistic regression of the relevant binary outcome (center shot chosen; GK stays) on leverage, with and without competition fixed effects, and (ii) non-parametric bootstrap / permutation tests (10,000 resamples) comparing baseline and crisis rates directly, which do not rely on the logistic model's distributional assumptions and are better suited to the modest cell sizes involved. For a one-sided permutation test of $H_1: \Delta_{\text{obs}} > 0$, the p-value is

$$ \hat{p} \;=\; \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B} \mathbb{1}\!\left[\, \Delta^{(b)} \ge \Delta_{\text{obs}} \,\right], \qquad B = 10{,}000, $$

with $\Delta^{(b)}$ computed after randomly reassigning kicks between the baseline/crisis labels within each bootstrap draw, and 95% confidence intervals obtained by the percentile method on within-group resampling.

4. Results

4.1 Center-shot usage falls under crisis (correct direction, underpowered)

The absolute drop of 3.4 percentage points (a 25.6% relative reduction) is in the hypothesized direction. A logistic regression of center-shot choice on leverage alone gives $\beta = -1.669$ ($p=0.224$), and the two-sample bootstrap permutation test (one-sided, $H_1:$ baseline rate $>$ crisis rate) gives an observed difference of $+0.034$ with 95% CI $[-0.032, +0.099]$ and $p=0.201$. Neither is significant at conventional thresholds. With only 43 center kicks in the entire dataset, the test is simply underpowered to reliably detect a difference of this size; we treat this result as directionally supportive but not independently conclusive, and return to how it should be weighted in Section 5.

4.2 Goalkeepers are significantly more likely to stay center under crisis

This is the paper's strongest statistical result. The bootstrap permutation test (one-sided, $H_1:$ crisis rate $>$ baseline rate) gives an observed difference of $+0.063$ with 95% CI $[+0.006, +0.122]$ — excluding zero — and $p=0.024$. A relative increase of 183% is both statistically and practically meaningful. The corresponding logistic regression, $\beta = +0.438$ ($p=0.826$), is not significant; with only 18 gk_stayed = 1 events across the full sample, the parametric model is severely underpowered relative to the non-parametric bootstrap, which uses the full resampling distribution rather than a single asymptotic approximation. We treat the bootstrap result as the more reliable of the two for this outcome and discuss the discrepancy further in Section 6.

4.3 Both groups remain broadly consistent with the literature MSNE

Using the goodness-of-fit statistic $\chi^2 = \sum_i (O_i - E_i)^2 / E_i$ with $\text{df}=2$, neither group significantly deviates from the literature MSNE, while both groups decisively reject the uninformative uniform benchmark ($p<0.001$). In other words, kickers in both regimes are recognizably playing a mixed strategy resembling professional equilibrium play — the deviations documented in Sections 4.1–4.2 are real but modest relative to the overall shape of the distribution, not a wholesale departure from strategic behavior.

4.4 The crisis deviation is targeted at Center, not a wholesale departure from MSNE

This is the paper's most counterintuitive result, and the one that most sharpens the thesis. Crisis kickers are, by the summary $L_2 = \sqrt{\sum_i (\hat p_i - p_i^{\text{lit}})^2}$ metric, closer to overall MSNE ($L_2 = 0.059$) than baseline kickers ($L_2 = 0.083$) — the reverse of what a naive "pressure degrades strategic play across the board" story would predict. A bootstrap test of deviation magnitude ($H_1:$ crisis deviates more than baseline) gives $p=0.653$, confirming the reversal is not a fluke of a single metric.

The reason is visible in Figure 4: crisis kickers match the literature Left/Right split almost exactly (0.414 vs. 0.415 on Left), whereas baseline kickers over-shoot toward the natural (Right) side (0.507 vs. 0.445). Crisis kickers' entire measured deviation from equilibrium is concentrated in the Center dimension — 0.099 vs. an MSNE-prescribed 0.140, a 29% shortfall. Framed this way, the finding is not that pressure erodes strategic rationality generally; it is that pressure produces a specific, targeted suppression of the one shot type whose failure mode (a Standing save) carries an outsized psychological cost. This is a sharper and more falsifiable claim than "kickers play worse under pressure," and it is the claim this paper defends.

4.5 The dual-failure signature: center value rises exactly as center usage falls

Binning kicks into five leverage quantiles ($n\approx70$ per bin) makes the mechanism concrete. The implied value of a center shot in each bin is

$$ \text{Implied center value} \;=\; 0.59 \times \text{(GK stay rate)} \;+\; 0.853 \times \big(1 - \text{GK stay rate}\big), $$

where $0.59$ is Palacios-Huerta's (2003) literature estimate of $P(\text{goal}\mid\text{center},\ \text{GK stays})$ and $0.853$ is this sample's empirical estimate of $P(\text{goal}\mid\text{center},\ \text{GK dives})$ (from the 34 center kicks with a diving goalkeeper). Because $0.853 \gg 0.59$ and goalkeepers stay center only a small fraction of the time in every bin, the implied value is dominated by the "GK dives" term and remains near 0.84 regardless of leverage — center shots are almost never the wrong choice given how goalkeepers actually behave. Center usage, meanwhile, falls with a linear trend slope of $-0.168$ across the same bins. Bin 4 (leverage 0.339–0.399) is a visible exception: a spike in GK stay rate to 12.1% (only 8 gk_stayed=1 events) temporarily pulls the implied value down to 0.821, which we treat as small-sample noise rather than a genuine reversal of the pattern (see Section 6).

4.6 The empty (Center, Center) cell is a sampling artifact, not a structural absence

Of the 43 center kicks in the sample, none were struck against a goalkeeper who stayed center (among the 17 center kicks with goalkeeper-direction data available). This is not evidence that the (Center, Center) outcome is structurally impossible: with an expected cell count of $(43/363)\times 17 \approx 2.0$ under independence, the probability of observing exactly zero by chance alone is 26.2%. We treat it as a sampling artifact of a small, sparse matrix (5 of 9 combined cells, and all 9 split-matrix cells, have $n<8$) rather than a substantive finding, and patch it with the literature prior of 0.59 wherever a downstream calculation requires it (Section 4.5). If anything, the pattern reinforces the broader thesis: on the rare occasions kickers do go center, goalkeepers have so far always dived away from it — further evidence that center shots are under-supplied relative to how rarely they are actually defended.

4.7 Summary of statistical tests

Five of seven directional predictions point the correct way; one test reaches conventional significance. This pattern — consistent direction, mostly modest magnitude, one clear exception — is exactly what we would expect if the dual-failure mechanism is real but the corpus is underpowered to detect every piece of it individually, which is the interpretive stance we defend in the next section.

5. Discussion: Defending the Dual-Failure Thesis

The core claim. Crisis kickers avoid center shots at a rate below the MSNE prescription: the literature benchmark is 14.0% center usage, crisis kickers in this sample shoot center only 9.9% of the time (a 29% shortfall against the benchmark), while baseline kickers (13.3%) sit much closer to the prescribed rate. Standing alone, this is a directionally consistent but statistically underpowered result (Section 4.1). What elevates it from a suggestive pattern to a defensible thesis is the goalkeeper-side evidence and the way the two sides interact.

The mechanism. Two distinct behavioral channels, acting on the two sides of the game, jointly generate the pattern:

  • Kickers — loss aversion. A missed penalty is psychologically costly, and a missed center shot specifically risks the "Standing save" — a save that requires no committed dive and is often perceived (rightly or wrongly) as more embarrassing for the kicker than a well-placed shot that is merely reached by a diving goalkeeper. This asymmetry in the felt cost of failure, orthogonal to the equal-leverage framing of the formal game, is exactly what should intensify under crisis conditions and push kickers toward the corners.
  • Goalkeepers — action bias. Bar-Eli et al. (2007) document that goalkeepers dive even when staying center is the expected-value-maximizing action, because inaction that fails is perceived as more blameworthy than a wrong guess that fails. Our data show this bias easing, not disappearing, under pressure: the stay rate nearly triples (3.4% → 9.7%), yet staying remains a small-minority response in both regimes. The point is not that goalkeepers overcome action bias in crisis — they do not — but that they lean against it slightly more than usual, in the one direction that matters for the kicker's decision problem.

Why "dual failure" is the right frame, rather than "kickers just choose worse under pressure." The two shifts are not independent curiosities; they compound. As leverage rises, goalkeepers become (modestly) more willing to defend the center — yet this is exactly when kickers most retreat from it. The implied value of a center shot remains pinned near 0.84 across every leverage bin (Section 4.5), meaning kickers are leaving value on the table precisely when the stakes, and therefore the cost of that lost value, are highest. A single-sided story ("kickers panic") would predict noisy, undirected departures from equilibrium across all three zones. What we observe instead is a coordinated, opposite-signed shift on both sides of the interaction, concentrated on the one axis the behavioral-economics literature would have predicted in advance.

Reconciling the counterintuitive Finding 4. The result that crisis kickers are, in aggregate $L_2$ terms, closer to the literature MSNE than baseline kickers (0.059 vs. 0.083) might appear to undercut the thesis. It does not: it sharpens it. Crisis kickers achieve near-perfect equilibrium compliance on the Left/Right margin (0.414 observed vs. 0.415 prescribed) while specifically under-supplying Center (0.099 vs. 0.140). A kicker under pressure is not becoming globally less rational; they are making a highly specific substitution — corner for center — that is fully consistent with an aversion to the one failure mode (the Standing save) that carries a distinctive psychological cost. This is a more precise, more falsifiable claim than a diffuse "pressure degrades strategic play," and it is the claim the data actually support.

Weighing the evidence honestly. The GK stay-rate result (Section 4.2, $p=0.024$, CI excluding zero) is the only individually significant test in the study, and it plays a specific evidentiary role: it establishes that goalkeeper behavior does shift measurably under pressure, which is the necessary condition for the dual-failure mechanism to operate at all. If goalkeepers were not changing their behavior, there would be no rising center-shot value for kickers to (fail to) exploit. The kicker-side result (Section 4.1, $p=0.201$) is directionally supportive but should not be oversold as independently conclusive; we present it, in keeping with the paper's overall stance, as consistent with the thesis rather than proof of it in isolation.

6. Limitations

  1. Sample size. $n=363$ kicks is the entirety of the StatsBomb open-data shootout corpus. The center-shot-specific analyses rest on only 43 center kicks, which limits the power of both the logistic regression and the bootstrap center-rate test in Section 4.1.
  2. Goalkeeper zone coverage. Goalkeeper dive direction is recoverable for only 18.2% of kicks (66/363), leaving the payoff matrix too sparse for a reliable data-driven MSNE estimate (Section 3). The broader goalkeeper analysis instead relies on the coarser gk_stayed binary, available for 82.6% of kicks, which identifies only whether the goalkeeper dived, not in which direction.
  3. Payoff-matrix degeneracy. The linear-programming solve for a data-driven MSNE returns a degenerate corner solution (100% GK-Right) due to sparsity. All chi-squared and deviation-magnitude tests therefore rely on the external Palacios-Huerta (2003) MSNE as reference; the strength of every "deviation from MSNE" claim in this paper is contingent on the continued validity of that external benchmark for the present sample.
  4. No kicker-quality controls. In-sample conversion rate is a poor proxy for kicker ability, since 336 of 363 kickers appear fewer than three times in the data. Reported effects are not adjusted for kicker quality or match context beyond leverage itself, leaving open the possibility that crisis kicks are systematically taken by a different mix of players (e.g., designated top-five specialists) than baseline kicks.
  5. Underpowered goalkeeper logit. Only 18 gk_stayed=1 events occur across the entire sample. The logistic regression for Hypothesis 2 is severely underpowered relative to the bootstrap test, and the discrepancy between the two ($p=0.826$ vs. $p=0.024$) should be read as a power difference between estimators, not as conflicting evidence.
  6. Bin 4 anomaly. The spike in goalkeeper stay rate in leverage bin 4 (0.339–0.399), driven by only 8 gk_stayed=1 events, disrupts the otherwise monotone decline in implied center-shot value shown in Figure 6. We treat this as small-sample noise, but cannot rule out an unobserved feature of that leverage range with more data.
  7. Competition heterogeneity. The sample spans 13 competitions and several decades of penalty-taking convention and goalkeeping technique. Fixed-effects regressions including competition indicators (not separately tabulated) show no statistically significant competition-level confounds for either hypothesis, but the sample is too small to rule them out definitively.

7. Conclusion & Future Work

This paper set out to test whether the Mixed-Strategy Nash Equilibrium behavior documented in the penalty-kick literature survives contact with genuine psychological pressure. Using a leverage measure derived from first-principles backward induction rather than a coarse proxy like "sudden death or not," we find suggestive evidence of a dual equilibrium failure: kickers under crisis conditions specifically and measurably retreat from center shots (13.3% → 9.9%), while goalkeepers simultaneously become significantly more willing to defend that same zone (3.4% → 9.7%, $p=0.024$). Because the implied value of a center shot remains high and roughly constant across the leverage spectrum, the two shifts compound: kickers leave the most value on the table exactly when the stakes, and the cost of that lost value, are greatest. A finer-grained decomposition shows this is not a wholesale abandonment of strategic play — crisis kickers track the literature equilibrium on the Left/Right margin about as well as, or better than, baseline kickers — but a targeted substitution away from the one shot type whose failure mode carries a distinctive psychological cost.

We view this analysis as a proof-of-concept on the full public StatsBomb corpus rather than a final word: several of the individual tests are underpowered, and the goalkeeper-direction data in particular are too sparse to support a fully data-driven equilibrium estimate. Three extensions follow directly from these limitations. First, a larger proprietary or manually-coded video dataset — targeting high-leverage World Cup and continental-championship knockout matches specifically — would directly address the small-cell problems in Sections 4.1, 4.5, and 4.6. Second, career-level kicker conversion rates (rather than the in-sample proxy used here) would allow the crisis/baseline comparison to control for the possibility that different kinds of players take high- versus low-leverage kicks. Third, and most directly actionable given the timing of this analysis, the ongoing 2026 FIFA World Cup offers a live, out-of-sample test: the historical model developed here generates a falsifiable prospective prediction — reduced center-shot usage and elevated goalkeeper stay rates in the tournament's highest-leverage shootout kicks — that can be checked against results as they occur, providing external validation independent of the historical sample used to build it.
