Executive Summary

    Model run: 2025-06-30 · Forecast window: Jul–Dec 2025
  


  

    This report documents a Regime-Switching Synthetic Forward Curve for Jet-A jet fuel,
    built for a European airline's Houston / US Gulf Coast fuel-uplift hub. Because no deeply

    using a Bayesian engine to translate weather, refinery, positioning and demand signals into

    02
    
Architecture: Regime-Switching Synthetic Forward Curve

    Two components, bridged by a Bayesian probability engine
  


  

    Physical Jet-A has no deeply liquid futures market, so the model is built from two pieces
    that do have rich, tradeable histories: a liquid macro benchmark (NYMEX Heating
    Oil / NY Harbor ULSD futures) and a localized physical basis (Gulf Coast Jet-A spot
    minus NY Harbor HO spot). Each forward month's Jet-A price is the benchmark curve plus a
    probability-weighted expected basis, where the probability comes from a dynamic
    Bayesian engine.

02.2Component 2 — Regime-Switched Basis

  

    The historical weekly basis (Jet-A spot − HO spot) is split into two physical regimes
    using a robust, outlier-resistant threshold over a 3-year trailing window:
  

  

    
Regime Threshold (upper-tail only)

    threshold = median(basis3Y) + 1.0 × 1.4826 × MAD(basis3Y)
  

  

    

      
Regime 0 — Normal

      
Weeks where basis ≤ threshold. Average spread under standard refinery operations / weather.

    

    

      
Regime 1 — Tail / Squeeze

      
Weeks where basis > threshold. Average spread during refinery outages, hurricanes, or localized distillate squeezes.

    

  

  

    This is a one-sided, upper-tail definition: it captures "Jet-A trades at an unusual
    premium to HO" events (the cost-risk scenario for the airline) but not symmetric
    Jet-A-discount blowouts. See Section 7 (COVID case study) and Section 11 for the implications.
  


  
02.3Bayesian Weather/Regime Probability Engine

  

    For each forward month, five indicators (refinery utilization, ENSO/ONI, Gulf Jet/HO
    managed-money positioning, TSA aviation-demand YoY, and a calendar-month seasonal factor)
    each produce a likelihood-ratio multiplier: 1.0 = neutral, >1.0 raises the
    probability of Regime 1, <1.0 lowers it. The engine combines these via odds-ratio
    updating — never by averaging multipliers directly.
  

  

    
Step A — Prior Odds

    Prior Odds = Pbase / (1 − Pbase)
  

  

    
Step B — Combined Multiplier (product of all active indicator LRs)

    Combined Multiplier = ∏i=1..n Mi
  

  

    
Step C — Posterior Odds

    Posterior Odds = Prior Odds × Combined Multiplier
  

  

    
Step D — Back to Probability

    Pfinal = Posterior Odds / (1 + Posterior Odds)   
    clamped to [0.0001, 0.9999]

02.5Hedging Reality — Imperfect Cross-Hedge

  

    To manage the macro exposure, the airline buys out-of-the-money HO call options as a
    cross-hedge. This protects against a broad energy-price spike but leaves the airline fully
    exposed to basis risk: if a localized Gulf Coast event spikes physical Jet-A while NY
    Harbor HO is flat or falling, the OTM calls expire worthless and the airline absorbs both the
    physical price spike and the option premium. The only true fixes — bespoke OTC
    Jet-A swaps or pre-purchasing physical fuel — are expensive and operationally heavy,
    which is precisely why this model exists: to size and time that residual basis risk.

    Houston/USGC hub → NY Harbor benchmark, via the Colonial Pipeline corridor

    NYMEX HO futures. Markers below show the modeled hub, the benchmark delivery point, three

03.1Why This Matters for the Model

  

    

      
PIPELINE CAPACITY CONSTRAINT

      

        Colonial Pipeline's distillate segments run near capacity in winter. When a Gulf Coast
        refinery outage coincides with strong Northeast heating demand, the pipeline cannot fully
        arbitrage the two markets — this is precisely the mechanism behind Regime-1 basis
        spikes (Section 4–5).
      

    

    

      
HURRICANE EXPOSURE

      

        All three highlighted Gulf Coast refining centers sit directly in the Atlantic
        hurricane corridor. Hurricane Harvey (2017, Section 7) shut down a large share of this
        capacity simultaneously — the model's seasonal factor for September reflects this
        concentrated risk.
      

    

    

      
HUB MISMATCH RISK

      

        NY Harbor HO and Houston Jet-A are ~2,500 km apart by pipeline. The 2022 distillate
        crisis (Section 7) shows these markets can decouple sharply — NY Harbor spiking on
        local inventory fears while Gulf Coast Jet-A stays comparatively anchored.

    07
    
Tail-Event Case Studies

    "Would our framework, calibrated on pre-event data only, have flagged this?"
  


  

    For each event, the regime classification (Section 4) is recomputed using only data
    available immediately before the event — a strict no-lookahead retrospective test.
    The resulting pre-event threshold is then compared against the basis path actually realized
    during the event. This is a stress test of the framework's own logic, not of the
    current 2025 calibration (that comes in Section 8).
  


  







  

    08
    
Stress Test of Current Assumptions

    "If a Section-7-magnitude shock hit during H2 2025, what would the model say?"
  


  

    Each scenario below takes the live 2025-06-30 calibration (Section 4) and shocks
    one indicator's underlying raw value to the historical extreme observed in the
    corresponding Section 7 case study, recomputes that indicator's z-score / empirical
    likelihood ratio against the current 3-year trailing window, and reruns the Bayesian
    engine for the affected month. Everything else — including the other four indicators
    — is held at its base-case 2025-06-30 value.
  


  


  

    CAVEAT The COVID scenario reveals a real limitation: the
    3-bin likelihood-ratio design saturates — a TSA YoY z-score of −1.17 (base case)
    and −13.28 (COVID-magnitude) fall in the same "weak_demand" bin and produce an
    identical multiplier, so the probability doesn't move at all despite an enormously
    larger shock. The compound La Niña scenario reveals a second: zero weeks in the
    current 3-year window have ONI≤−1.0, so that indicator's "strong_la_nina"
    likelihood ratio (3.35) is a pure Laplace-smoothing extrapolation with no supporting
    observations. Both are flagged as model-fragility findings in Section 11.

    10
    
Diagnosis

    Synthesis across Sections 6–9
  


  
10.1What the Model Got Right

  

    

      
THE BASIS MODEL IS THE STRONG PART

      

        Across 66 backtest observations, basis_MAE (0.066 $/gal) is roughly
        4× smaller than benchmark_MAE (0.284 $/gal). The regime-switching
        basis component is doing its job — the dominant source of forecast error is the
        much simpler benchmark HO curve (a front-month price plus a fixed seasonal shape), not
        the part of the architecture this project focused on.
      

    

    

      
H2 2025 OUTPERFORMED THE HISTORICAL AVERAGE

      

        The live Jul–Dec 2025 forecast achieved a total MAE of 0.176 $/gal, beating
        the 11-year backtest average of 0.264 $/gal, with a basis MAE of just
        0.058 $/gal. The model correctly identified September as the highest-risk month
        (24.5% P(Regime1)) and correctly predicted no realized Regime-1 month — the
        closest approach was a basis of +0.044 on Oct 10, against a threshold of
        0.0737, coinciding with a USGC refinery utilization dip to 87.1%. The mechanism
        (refinery utilization → basis) is responding to the right signal, even though no
        week crossed the bar in 2025.
      

    

    

      
RETROSPECTIVE STRESS TESTS CONFIRM THE LOGIC

      

        Section 7's no-lookahead retrospective tests show the framework would have caught
        Hurricane Harvey (4/9 weeks flagged, peak basis 0.274 vs. threshold 0.037) using only
        pre-event data, and correctly stayed quiet for the 2014 Polar Vortex and Winter Storm
        Uri — both severe cold/demand events that did not produce Jet-A-premium
        basis blowouts. The regime definition is doing the right thing for the events it is
        designed to catch.
      

    

    

      
FORWARD STRESS TESTS PRODUCE SENSIBLE, LARGE RESPONSES

      

        Applying a Harvey-magnitude refinery shock to September 2025 more than doubles
        P(Regime1) (24.5% → 51.9%); an Uri-magnitude shock to December nearly triples it
        (8.8% → 24.2%); and a compound La Niña + refinery-outage shock to December
        produces the largest single move in the report (8.8% → 48.0%). The engine is
        appropriately sensitive to the inputs it was designed around.
      

    

  


  
10.2What the Model Got Wrong — and Why

  

    

      
DECEMBER 2025: A BENCHMARK MISS, NOT A BASIS MISS

      

        December's total error of +0.322 $/gal — the worst single month — was
        driven almost entirely by benchmark_error (+0.260). The seasonal
        shape (fit on 2014–2023 June-30 curves) implies a modest Dec uptick from the June
        front-month price ($2.346 → $2.359), but actual HO fell sharply to $2.099.
        basis_error for December was a comparatively modest +0.062. This is a
        benchmark-curve-shape problem, not a regime-model problem.
      

    

    

      
THE REGIME DEFINITION IS ONE-SIDED BY DESIGN

      

        Both COVID 2020 (basis to −0.364) and the core of the 2022 distillate crisis
        (basis to −1.013) were Jet-A-discount blowouts — the mirror image of
        what Regime 1 is defined to catch. The framework either misses these entirely or, in
        COVID's case, flags a coincidental small positive blip for the wrong reason. Any
        treasury use of P(Regime1) should be paired with a separate qualitative watch for
        demand-collapse / hub-decoupling scenarios that this number does not cover.
      

    

    

      
MILD OVERCONFIDENCE IN THE TAIL BASE RATE

      

        Over 2014–2024, the model's mean predicted P(Regime1) was 23.3% vs. an
        actual realized rate of 15.2% — regime_prob_MAE of 0.315.
        2025 was better calibrated (mean predicted 15.7% vs. actual 0%, MAE 0.157), but the
        historical pattern suggests the climatological base rate (and/or the indicator
        multipliers) run slightly hot on average.
      

    

    

      
DISCRETE LIKELIHOOD RATIOS CAN SATURATE OR EXTRAPOLATE BLINDLY

      

        The COVID stress test (Section 8) shows the 3-bin design cannot distinguish a 1.2σ
        demand dip from a 13.3σ collapse — both land in "weak_demand" with an
        identical multiplier. Separately, the compound La Niña scenario's
        "strong_la_nina" likelihood ratio (3.35) rests on zero observations in the
        current 3-year window — it is pure Laplace-smoothing extrapolation. Both are
        latent fragilities that only surface under stress, not in the base case.
      

    

  


  
10.3Net Assessment

  

    The core regime-switching basis architecture is sound and validated: it is a small
    contributor to overall error, it responds sensibly to stress, and its retrospective
    case-study performance matches physical intuition (refinery-outage events caught,
    pure cold-snap/demand events correctly ignored). The dominant sources of forecast error
    — the benchmark HO curve shape and the asymmetric regime definition — are both
    well-understood, well-isolated, and directly actionable, and form the basis of the
    ranked improvements in Section 11.
  







  

    11
    
Proposed Improvements, Ranked by Impact

    8 items · derived from Sections 6–10
  


  

    

      
1

      

        
Implement the true Rotterdam / ARA — ICE Gasoil hub

        

          The Houston/USGC–NY Harbor pairing is a U.S. data-availability proxy for a
          European airline's actual European uplift points. The 2022 case study (Section 7)
          shows the chosen benchmark hub can decouple sharply from the physical delivery
          point (NY Harbor HO spiked to $4.55 while Gulf Coast Jet-A stayed near $3.3–4.0)
          — the exact hub-mismatch risk a European carrier would actually face. Re-running
          the entire pipeline on ICE Gasoil futures + Rotterdam/ARA Jet-A barges would directly
          address the airline's real exposure.
        

      

      
HIGH IMPACT
data availability

    


    

      
2

      

        
Add a symmetric (two-sided) regime definition

        

          The current upper-tail-only definition misses both COVID 2020 (basis −0.364)
          and the core of the 2022 crisis (basis −1.013) — the two largest moves in
          the dataset after Harvey. A "Regime −1" (Jet-A discount / demand collapse or
          hub-decoupling) defined symmetrically around the median, with its own Bayesian
          probability, would give the treasury team a genuinely two-sided risk picture instead
          of a one-sided "cost risk only" view.
        

      

      
HIGH IMPACT
model design

    


    

      
3

      

        
Replace 3-bin likelihood ratios with continuous (kernel or logistic) functions

        

          Section 8's COVID stress test shows the discrete low/mid/high bin design cannot
          distinguish a 1.2σ shock from a 13.3σ shock — both saturate at the
          same multiplier. A continuous likelihood function (e.g., a fitted logistic curve or
          kernel density ratio over z-score) would let extreme shocks produce proportionally
          extreme probability moves, which matters most exactly when it matters most.
        

      

      
HIGH IMPACT
Bayesian engine

    


    

      
4

      

        
Add a dedicated Heating-Degree-Days (HDD) series

        

          Both cold-snap case studies (Polar Vortex 2014, Uri 2021) were driven by Northeast
          demand, not Gulf Coast supply — yet the model has no direct demand-side weather
          indicator. An HDD anomaly series for the NY Harbor delivery region would let the
          model distinguish "benchmark-level demand shock" from "Gulf Coast supply shock"
          ex-ante, rather than only seeing it after the fact in the benchmark curve.
        

      

      
MEDIUM IMPACT
new data source

    


    

      
5

      

        
Re-estimate the seasonal forward-curve shape per backtest year (expanding window)

        

          The seasonal Jun30→{Jul..Dec} shape is fit once on 2014–2023 data and reused
          for every backtest year, including 2014 itself — a minor look-ahead for early
          years. Since benchmark_error is the single largest error source
          (Section 6), refitting the shape on an expanding window (only data available as of
          each as-of date) would tighten the backtest's validity and may reveal whether the
          shape itself is drifting over time.
        

      

      
MEDIUM IMPACT
backtest rigor

    


    

      
6

      

        
Regularize small-sample indicator bins (shrink toward neutral)

        

          The compound La Niña stress scenario (Section 8) relies on a "strong_la_nina"
          likelihood ratio of 3.35 backed by zero observations in the current 3-year
          window — a pure artifact of Laplace smoothing. A confidence-weighted shrinkage
          (multiplier → 1.0 as supporting sample size → 0) would prevent rarely-seen
          ENSO states from producing outsized, poorly-supported probability swings.
        

      

      
MEDIUM IMPACT
Bayesian engine

    


    

      
7

      

        
Fix the ENSO season-to-date "center month" convention

        

          ONI seasons are assigned to their center month with no additional publication lag
          (e.g., MJJ 2025 → June 2025), which exactly reproduces the validated reference
          numbers but is technically a ~6-week look-ahead vs. NOAA's real publication schedule.
          Switching to an end-of-season-plus-lag convention would be more conservative; the
          impact on the 5-indicator product is likely small but should be quantified.
        

      

      
LOW IMPACT
data hygiene

    


    

      
8

      

        
Re-examine the Jet/HO COT positioning indicator's sign

        

          The 2022-crisis stress scenario shows "bullish_positioning" (managed money net long
          the Jet/HO spread) carrying a likelihood ratio of 0.578 — i.e., historically
          associated with lower Regime-1 probability, the opposite of the naive prior.
          This may reflect genuine contrarian/mean-reversion dynamics, or it may be small-sample
          noise (157-week window). Worth a dedicated study with a longer COT history or an
          alternative positioning measure before relying on it in a stress scenario.
        

      

      
LOW IMPACT
indicator design
