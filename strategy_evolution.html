Section 01

Executive Summary & Challenge Overview

Round 1 of IMC Prosperity 4 introduced two instruments — Ash-Coated Osmium (ASH_COATED_OSMIUM) and Intarian Pepper Root (INTARIAN_PEPPER_ROOT) — with ±80 unit position limits. The submitted production algorithm generated 95,344.28 XIRECs over 1,000,000 out-of-sample ticks (10 trading days), decomposing into 16,365 from Osmium market-making and 78,788 from Pepper Root directional trend capture. Maximum drawdown from high-water mark was 674 XIRECs, occurring during the initial Pepper Root position build in the first 4,000 ticks.

This memo traces the path from my initial brainstorming (documented in the Rough Ideas PDF, where I first identified the core regime difference) through seven iterative backtests, three architectural rewrites, and the production deployment. The PnL improvement was non-monotonic — attempt 3 actually regressed from attempt 2, teaching a hard lesson about over-fitting signal confirmation gates.

Section 02

Market Microstructure & Initial Hypotheses

My initial research (documented in the Rough Ideas PDF before any code was written) identified the fundamental regime divergence between the two instruments. I wrote at the time:

From Rough Ideas, verbatim: "This product [Osmium] is clearly similar to the emeralds from round 0. The price is bound to 10,000, but unlike emeralds, osmium is a lot more volatile. Our strategy should therefore be similar to what we had for emeralds." And for Pepper Root: "Tried an EMA, and it did shit. As you can see, strong trend-upwards… the simple thing to do here was to use a linear regression."

This was the correct diagnosis from day one. The initial statistical work confirmed it: Osmium exhibited negative autocorrelation (mean change after a positive tick was −12 to −20 units, and +20 to +35 after a negative tick — classic mean-reversion), while Pepper Root drifted +1,000 units per trading day with near-mechanical linearity.

The early research note correctly identified that an EMA would "constantly lag behind a trending price" because it anchors to historical averages. For a non-stationary series, the EMA's fair value estimate trails the actual price — the bot systematically sells into a rally and buys into a crash. This is the core pathology I spent seven iterations resolving.

Section 03

The "Graveyard" — Live-Market Failures

3.1 — Flaw A: EMA on a Trending Asset (Attempt 1)

The first attempt used an EMA with α=0.19 on both products. On Osmium, this worked acceptably — the EMA tracked the stationary mean and the bot captured spread. On Pepper Root, it was a disaster in waiting. An EMA with α=0.19 has an effective half-life of ~3.3 ticks. As the price drifted upward at ~0.10/tick, the EMA lagged by approximately 0.10 × 3.3 ≈ 0.33 ticks per step — enough to cause the bot to systematically sell below fair value as the trend accelerated.

attempt1.py — the flawed unified approach# Both assets used the same EMA logic. Pepper Root's trend made this lethal. alpha = self.alphas.get(product, 0.1) # PEP: 0.19, OSM: 0.11 self.emas[product] = alpha * mid_price + (1 - alpha) * self.emas[product] # Position skew: pos/8 — weak, on ±20 limits this maxes at ±2 ticks position_skew = int(position / 8)

Result: 2,370 XIRECs. Most of this came from Osmium. Pepper Root contributed 855 but was leaving massive alpha on the table by selling into the trend.

3.2 — Flaw B: The Expanding Session Mean Drag

The more subtle version of this pathology appears when using a longer lookback. An expanding session mean (or very long SMA) on Pepper Root is mathematically guaranteed to underperform. As the price moves linearly from P₀ to P₀+nΔ, the session mean at tick k is:

Session Mean vs. True Price — The Drag Equation session_mean(k) = P₀ + (k/2)·Δ
actual_price(k) = P₀ + k·Δ
drag(k) = k·Δ/2 ← grows linearly with time. By tick 10,000 the mean is 500+ ticks behind.

Why this kills a market-maker: When the fair value estimate (red) lags the actual price (teal), the bot's ask price sits below the market. Live bots hit that ask immediately, filling us short into a rising market. We accumulate toxic short inventory that increases losses with every tick the trend continues. This is textbook adverse selection — informed flow runs over our stale quotes.

3.3 — Flaw C: OBI Gate Over-Filtering (Attempt 3)

Attempt 3 added an Order Book Imbalance (OBI) confirmation gate to the Pepper Root taker strategy. The idea was sound in theory — require both a favorable regression slope and favorable volume imbalance before taking. In practice, the OBI gate filtered out ~60% of valid entry signals. The regression alone was correct; adding a noisy confirmation just reduced fill rate.

attempt3.py — the over-filtered taker# Required THREE conditions. Too restrictive — missed most of the trend. if pred_price > best_ask + 1 and slope > 0.5 and obi > 0.1: buy_vol = limit - position # This almost never fires. The OBI signal is too noisy.

Result: PnL dropped from 2,911 to 2,496 — a 14% regression. I removed the OBI gate in attempt 4, which immediately recovered the lost performance. Lesson: don't add confirmation signals that are noisier than your primary signal. The slope from a 15-tick regression already had statistical meaning; the OBI at a single snapshot did not.

3.4 — Flaw D: ±20 Position Limits (Attempts 1–5)

For the first five iterations, I used position limits of ±20. The actual exchange limit was ±80. This meant the algorithm was operating at 25% of available capacity — throttling its PnL by a factor of 4× while incurring the same code complexity. When attempt 6 switched to ±80 limits, the PnL immediately jumped from 2,911 to 3,892 despite other changes being made simultaneously. The ±20 limit was the single largest systematic drag on performance across the first five iterations.

Section 04

Iterative Refinements & Production Logic

4.1 — The Breakthrough: Attempt 6 (Architecture Rewrite)

Attempt 6 was a ground-up rewrite with three structural changes: (1) ±80 limits, (2) JSON-serialized persistent state across ticks, and (3) completely new signal generation. For Osmium, I replaced the simple EMA with a 250-tick SMA oscillator designed to exploit the sine-wave-like cycling pattern. For Pepper Root, I introduced regime switching based on a 100-tick slope detector:

attempt6.py — regime switching for PEPslope = (pepper_hist[-1] - pepper_hist[-lookback]) / lookback if slope > 0.08: # BULL → accumulate, minimal skew pred_price += 4.0 position_skew = (c_pos / limit) * 1.0 elif slope < -0.08: # BEAR → dump, short bias pred_price -= 4.0 else: # NEUTRAL → standard spread capture position_skew = (c_pos / limit) * 3.0

This was the first iteration that correctly separated "trend-following mode" from "market-making mode" on Pepper Root. When the slope exceeded 0.08 ticks/step, the algorithm shifted quotes upward by 4 ticks, stopped skewing against its position, and even moved the ask 10 ticks above market to prevent accidental selling into the trend. Result: 3,892 XIRECs (+34% over the stalled attempts 2–5).

Three specific innovations in the production code:

1. The t-statistic gate replaced the crude slope > 0.08 threshold. Instead of asking "is there a trend?", it asks "is this slope estimate statistically distinguishable from zero?" This requires a minimum of 30 observations (PEP_MIN_N=30) and a t-stat exceeding 2.5. This is more conservative than attempt 6's slope gate but far more reliable — it won't fire on noise.

2. The tanh bias saturation: dynamic_bias = 3.5 × tanh(50 × slope). The tanh function saturates near ±3.5 for any meaningfully non-zero slope. This clips the maximum forward projection — the fair value can never be more than ~7 ticks (3.5 bias + 3.5×slope) ahead of mid, preventing runaway extrapolation.

3. The shock filter on Osmium: a weighted average of Δ1, Δ2, and Δ4 (recent price changes over 1, 2, and 4 ticks), subtracted from fair value. This is an anti-momentum signal — when prices have just risen, the algorithm expects mean-reversion and adjusts downward. The weights (0.55, 0.30, 0.15) decay with lookback, giving the most recent move the strongest signal.

Section 05

Risk Profiling & Tail-Risk Analysis

Traditional metrics like the Sharpe ratio are misleading for market-making strategies because they assume normally distributed returns. A market-maker's risk profile is dominated by two tail events: adverse selection (informed flow runs over stale quotes) and inventory limit breach (position hits the hard cap and the bot can only trade in one direction). I evaluate the production algorithm against these specific risks.

5.1 — Inventory Variance: The Asymmetric Skew

The OSM strategy uses a position-dependent skew of (pos/80) × 3.0. This means at maximum position (+80), both bid and ask are shifted by 3 ticks toward rebalancing. The mathematical consequence is that the position follows a mean-reverting process around zero:

OSM Inventory Dynamics At position +80: skew = +3.0 → bid at fair−4, ask at fair−2
→ Sell orders are 2 ticks more aggressive than buys
→ Sells fill preferentially → position drifts toward zero

At position −80: skew = −3.0 → bid at fair+2, ask at fair+4
→ Buy orders are 2 ticks more aggressive than sells
→ Buys fill preferentially → position drifts toward zero

In the OOS backtest, the OSM position never exceeded ±50 units (out of the ±80 limit), with the vast majority of time spent in the ±20 range. The skew parameter (3.0) provided sufficient restoring force without being so aggressive that it sacrificed spread capture.

5.2 — Adverse Selection Monitoring

The shock filter on OSM directly addresses adverse selection. By subtracting weighted recent momentum from fair value, the algorithm fades short-term moves rather than chasing them. When an informed trader hits our resting buy order, the price has typically just dropped — meaning our buy is at a locally good price, and we expect reversion. The negative autocorrelation of OSM (confirmed at −12 to −20 ticks per positive move) validates this: the counter-party flow we're absorbing is, on average, uninformed noise.

6.2 — Capture Ratio

The OSM market-making strategy captured an average of 14.83 XIRECs per round-trip against a market bid-ask spread averaging ~16.25 XIRECs. This is a capture ratio of 91.3% — meaning we forfeited only ~1.42 XIRECs per round-trip to the adverse selection cost of our inventory skew. This is a high-quality result for a fully automated market-maker operating without any information about counterparty identity.

The PEP directional strategy's "capture ratio" is effectively the percentage of the total trend extracted. PEP drifted ~10,000 ticks per 10 days (1,000/day × 10). At 80 units, the theoretical maximum capture is 80 × 10,000 = 800,000 XIRECs. Our actual capture was 78,788 — a 9.8% theoretical extraction rate. The gap comes from the initial cost of entry (~13 ticks above optimal) and the lack of partial profit-taking.

Section 07

Post-Mortem & Residual Risks

What Worked

The core regime identification was correct from Day 1. Osmium is stationary; Pepper Root trends. The production algorithm correctly applied mean-reversion market-making to Osmium and directional trend-following to Pepper Root. The t-statistic gating on Pepper Root was the single highest-value innovation — it replaced crude threshold-based slope detection with a statistically principled confidence test, dramatically reducing false entries while preserving sensitivity to real trends.

The OSM shock filter's anti-momentum behavior was well-matched to the instrument's negative autocorrelation structure. The 91.3% spread capture ratio confirms that the algorithm was not being systematically adversely selected.

7.1 — What Still Doesn't Work

⚠ No Stop-Loss on PEP

The algorithm has zero downside protection on its +80 long position. If PEP's trend reverses, losses compound at 80× the price movement. The initial −674 drawdown is a preview of this risk. Fix: implement a trailing stop or conditional position reduction when the t-stat flips sign for N consecutive ticks.

⚠ Binary PEP Entry

The algorithm goes from 0 to +80 in 300 ticks (ts 3,400–3,700). This all-or-nothing entry creates maximum timing risk. Fix: staged entry — 25→50→80 with required re-confirmation of t-stat > 2.5 at each stage.

⚠ OSM Spread is Static

The ±1 tick spread offset is hardcoded. In high-volatility regimes (when the shock filter detects large Δ values), the spread should widen to compensate for increased adverse selection risk. Conversely, in calm markets, tighter spreads would increase fill rate. Fix: spread = max(1, int(abs(shock) * 0.8)).

⚠ No PEP Profit-Taking

The algorithm never sells PEP. It enters +80 early and holds to session end. For a 10-day session, this means carrying unrealized gains of 70,000+ XIRECs with zero crystallization. Fix: partial profit-taking at configurable intervals (e.g., sell 20 units every 100,000 ticks and re-buy on dips).

ℹ Regime-Change Blindness

The PEP strategy is implicitly a bet on trend continuation. It does not model the possibility that PEP's regime could switch from trending to mean-reverting mid-session. A Markov regime-switching model could address this.

ℹ OSM High Market Share

In the 1-day backtest, 56 of 61 OSM trades (92%) involved our submission. If the counterparties are consistently more informed, this high participation rate becomes a liability rather than an asset. Monitor post-fill mid-price movement to detect toxic flow.

7.2 — Proposed Fixes for Round 2

Concrete Mathematical Improvements 1. PEP trailing stop: if t_stat < -1.5 for 10 consecutive ticks while long, reduce position by 50%
2. Staged PEP entry: [0→25] at t>2.5, [25→50] at t>2.0 after 500 ticks, [50→80] at t>1.5 after 1000 ticks
3. OSM dynamic spread: spread_offset = max(1, round(0.8 × |shock|))
4. PEP partial profit-taking: sell 20% of position every time unrealized gain exceeds 2,000 XIRECs
5. OSM adverse selection monitor: track mid_price[t+5] − fill_price for all fills; if consistently negative, widen quotes
