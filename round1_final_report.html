Round 1 presented two instruments with structurally opposite price dynamics. Osmium is stationary and mean-reverting around ~10,000, suited for two-sided passive quoting. Pepper Root exhibits a near-deterministic linear drift of +1,000 ticks per trading day (slope ≈ 0.1033 per tick, R² > 0.99), suited for directional accumulation. Identifying this regime divergence was the first and most important step.

1.1 — Osmium Periodicity Investigation

While analysing the in-sample price data, I ran a deeper investigation into Osmium's microstructure using autocorrelation analysis and FFT spectral decomposition. The ACF showed clear wave-like echoes, and the FFT identified a dominant cycle at ~3,949 ticks — confirming the price follows a quasi-sinusoidal oscillation around 10,000.

I then fitted a sine wave directly to the first 1,000 ticks via scipy.optimize.curve_fit. The result confirmed that Osmium's volatility is not random noise but has exploitable periodic structure. This insight reinforced the decision to use a short-window SMA (rather than a longer lookback) to track the local phase of the oscillation.

Section 02

The Fair Value Problem

The central technical challenge for Pepper Root was computing a fair value that doesn't lag behind the trend. An expanding session mean or slow EMA produces a systematically biased estimate — the bot's ask sits below market, and informed bots hit it immediately. The drag grows linearly with time:

Session Mean Drag on a Linear Trend session_mean(k) = P₀ + (k/2)·Δ
actual_price(k) = P₀ + k·Δ
drag(k) = k·Δ/2 ← by tick 10,000, the mean is 500+ ticks behind market

Why this is fatal for a market-maker: When the fair value estimate (red) lags the actual price (teal), our ask price sits below market. Informed counterparties hit it immediately, filling us short into a rising market. This is textbook adverse selection. Only the OLS regression (gold) stays centred on the true price trajectory.

3.1 — Key Failure: The OBI Trap (Attempt 3)

Attempt 3 added an Order Book Imbalance (OBI) gate as a second confirmation signal: only take if slope > 0.5 AND obi > 0.1. OBI at single-tick resolution is dominated by noise. It filtered ~60% of valid trend entries. PnL dropped 14%. Removing it in attempt 4 immediately recovered the loss. Lesson: never add a confirmation signal noisier than your primary signal.

3.2 — Key Failure: ±20 Limit Throttle (Attempts 1–5)

For five iterations I used position limits of ±20. The exchange allowed ±80. The algorithm was running at 25% capacity. The ±80 fix in attempt 6 caused an immediate +34% PnL jump — the single largest systematic drag of the early iterations.

Section 04

Production Strategy Architecture

4.1 — OSM: Composite Market-Maker

The Osmium sub-strategy posts two-sided quotes around a composite fair value. The fair value blends a short-window SMA (tracking the local oscillation phase) with a slow global EMA (anchoring long-run mean), then subtracts a weighted momentum shock signal to fade short-term moves — directly exploiting the negative autocorrelation.

OSM Signal Pipeline
Local SMA (25)×0.65
+
Global EMA (α=0.001)×0.35
−
Shock Filter0.55·Δ1+0.30·Δ2+0.15·Δ4
→
Fair ± 1 − skewBid / Ask
OSM Quoting & Inventory Skew fair = 0.65·local_sma + 0.35·global_target − 0.25·shock
skew = (position / 80) × 3.0
bid_px = round(fair − 1 − skew) ← capped at best_bid + 1
ask_px = round(fair + 1 − skew) ← floored at best_ask − 1
Execution positioning: The min(bid_px, best_bid + 1) and max(ask_px, best_ask - 1) constraints ensure our quotes are always competitive with the existing order book. We penny the BBO — posting 1 tick inside the best bid and ask — to attract fills from live bots while preserving a positive expected spread. Without this, resting orders sit behind the queue and rarely fill.

4.2 — PEP: Statistically-Gated Trend Follower

The Pepper Root sub-strategy runs a 50-tick rolling OLS regression and computes the slope's t-statistic. When |t| exceeds 2.5 (statistically significant trend), it takes aggressive directional fills at the BBO. Otherwise, it posts passive quotes around a bias-adjusted fair value.

The tanh(50·slope) bias saturates at ±3.5, preventing unbounded extrapolation. The t-stat gate requires n ≥ 30 observations — the algorithm won't act on a slope estimate from too few points.

Section 07

Post-Mortem & Forward Improvements

Assessment

The regime identification was correct from Day 1 and the production algorithm was well-matched to both instruments. The t-statistic gate was the single highest-value innovation — replacing a crude slope threshold with a statistically principled confidence test. The 91.3% spread capture ratio on OSM and the ×10.02 OOS scaling confirm that the algorithm generalises without overfitting.

The initial −674 drawdown at ts 4,000 (during PEP position build) is the main risk event. It resolved within 8,000 ticks, but the lack of a stop-loss means a regime change could compound this loss.

✗ No Stop-Loss on PEP

+80 long with zero downside protection. If PEP reverses, losses compound at 80× the price move. Fix: trailing stop when t_stat < −1.5 for 10 consecutive ticks.

✗ Binary PEP Entry

0 → +80 in 300 ticks. Maximum timing risk. Fix: staged entry (25→50→80) with re-confirmation at each stage.

⚠ Static OSM Spread

±1 tick hardcoded. Should widen in high-vol, tighten in calm conditions. Fix: spread = max(1, round(0.8 × |shock|)).

⚠ No Profit Crystallisation

PEP position held to session end. 70,000+ XIRECs unrealised. Fix: partial take-profit at configurable thresholds.
