Section 03

Strategy Logic & Design

The algorithm is architecturally clean: a single Trader.run() loop dispatches to one of two distinct sub-strategies based on the active product symbol. State is persisted across ticks via a JSON-serialised memory object. The key design principle is regime-first thinking — the algorithm does not attempt a unified approach; it tailors logic tightly to each instrument's empirical behaviour.

3.1 — Ash-Coated Osmium: Mean-Reversion Market-Maker

The Osmium sub-strategy is a two-sided quote market-maker with fair-value estimation driven by a composite of local short-window SMA, a slowly-updating global anchor, and a momentum shock filter. The objective is to post tight two-sided quotes around an estimated fair value and capture the bid-ask spread repeatedly across many round-trips.

skew = (position / 80) × 3.0
bid_px = fair − 1 − skew
ask_px = fair + 1 − skew

Several design choices merit explanation. The 65/35 weighting between local SMA and global target means that recent price action dominates fair value, but a slow-moving global anchor prevents the estimate from drifting too far from a long-run mean. The global EMA uses an extremely slow alpha (0.001) to resist transient dislocations.

The shock filter acts as a momentum-reversal signal: when prices have recently risen (positive Δ1/Δ2/Δ4), the fair value is adjusted downward — i.e., the algorithm expects mean-reversion and quotes slightly lower to capture the anticipated reversal. This is directly informed by the negative autocorrelation structure of OSM's price process.

The inventory skew is the risk management mechanism. When the algorithm is long (positive position), both the bid and ask are shifted downward, biasing fills toward selling. When short, they shift upward, biasing toward buying. At maximum position (±80), the skew is ±3 ticks, creating a meaningful bias to rebalance.

Shrinkage cold-start: During the first 100 ticks, the global_target transitions from the opening mid price toward the long-run EMA via a linear shrinkage parameter. This prevents the algorithm from over-weighting an uninformative starting observation when the position history is short.

3.2 — Intarian Pepper Root: Trend-Following with Statistical Gating

The Pepper Root sub-strategy uses rolling OLS regression over a 50-tick window to estimate the current price trend slope. A t-statistic is computed for that slope estimate. Depending on whether the t-stat exceeds a threshold, the algorithm switches between two modes:

Mode A — Trend Regime (|t| > 2.5)
When the OLS slope is statistically significant, the algorithm abandons market-making and takes directional liquidity — hitting the best ask to go long (t > 0) or hitting the best bid to go short (t < 0) at maximum position.
Mode B — Ranging Regime (|t| ≤ 2.5)
When no significant trend is detected, the algorithm reverts to passive quote posting around a fair value augmented by a slope-adjusted bias term. This avoids overpaying for liquidity in choppy conditions.
PEP Fair Value and Signal slope, slope_std = OLS(mid_prices[-50:]) ← rolling linear regression
t_stat = slope / slope_std

dynamic_bias = 3.5 × tanh(50 × slope) ← saturating bias, sign follows slope
fair_pep = mid + dynamic_bias + (3.5 × slope) ← forward-adjusted fair value

if |t_stat| > 2.5:
    → Aggressive fill at best bid/ask (full position)
else:
    → Passive quotes at fair_pep ± 1 − skew

The tanh(50 × slope) bias term is particularly thoughtful: it saturates quickly near ±3.5 for any meaningfully non-zero slope, creating a hard-clip on forward projection. This prevents the fair value estimate from becoming arbitrarily extrapolated in a runaway trend. The additional linear slope multiplier of 3.5× then adds a smaller proportional trend projection on top.

Why OLS regression rather than simple price comparison? The t-statistic gating provides statistical confidence in the slope estimate — not just its sign, but its reliability given residual variance. The minimum window of 30 ticks ensures the algorithm doesn't act on a slope computed from just a handful of points, which would be noise-dominated.

Section 05

Risk Assessment

Despite the positive outcome, a candid assessment of risks embedded in this design is essential for forward-looking iteration.

⚠ PEP Entry Timing Risk

The algorithm entered max long at a local price peak (12,012–12,013), immediately before a ~9-point dip. Had the trend reversed rather than resumed, the early -679 XIREC drawdown could have compounded. The strategy has no stop-loss mechanism on the directional leg.

⚠ Residual Open Positions

At session end: +80 PEP long and −6 OSM short remain open. The PEP position carries mark-to-market risk between sessions. If PEP reverses, the 7,009 XIREC in P&L is partially unrealized and vulnerable.

✓ OSM Inventory Control

The inventory skew mechanism performed as intended — OSM position never exceeded ±23 units across the session, well within the ±80 limit. The skew parameter (3.0) provided sufficient restoring force without over-constraining quote aggressiveness.

✓ No Adverse Selection Events

The shock filter on OSM successfully dampened momentum: no evidence of being caught on the wrong side of a sustained OSM price move. The mean-reverting dynamics were correctly anticipated and consistently exploited.

ℹ PEP Strategy is Regime-Dependent

The PEP strategy is implicitly a bet on continued drift. The OLS approach detects trend but does not model potential regime changes (e.g., if PEP becomes mean-reverting). Performance could degrade sharply if the trend breaks mid-round.

ℹ OSM: Market-Maker Concentration

56 of 61 OSM trades involved our submission, implying very high market share. While this demonstrates fill quality, it also suggests the algorithm may be providing most of OSM's liquidity — potentially being adversely selected by better-informed counterparties.

Section 06

Conclusions & Forward Recommendations

Overall Assessment

The Round 1 strategy performed strongly given the regime it encountered. The correct identification of OSM as mean-reverting and PEP as trending was the primary alpha source, and the algorithm was appropriately calibrated for both. The 75%/25% PnL split (PEP/OSM) reflects the dominant contribution of the directional leg, which benefited both from correct signal detection and from riding a persistent, low-noise trend.

The early PEP drawdown (−679 XIREC at ts 3,800) is the most notable execution risk realised in this round. It resolved favourably, but highlights the need for a more controlled entry mechanism — potentially spreading the accumulation over more fills, or waiting for a confirmed retrace before committing to full position.

OSM market-making was consistent and well-managed. Capturing an average of 14.83 XIREC per round-trip against a ~16.25 spread is a high-quality result. The skew mechanism successfully kept inventory bounded, and the shock filter prevented chasing adverse momentum.

Recommended Improvements for Round 2

→ PEP: Staged Entry Protocol

Rather than filling the full 80-unit position in 5 aggressive ticks, implement a staged entry (e.g., 25→50→80) with confirmation between stages to reduce entry timing risk.

→ PEP: Partial Profit-Taking

If the trend has accumulated significant unrealised gains, consider scaling out to realise profits before the session ends, rather than carrying maximum exposure to the final mark.

→ OSM: Dynamic Spread Adjustment

The fixed ±1 tick spread is simple but could be widened in high-volatility regimes (when shock is large) to earn more per fill, and tightened in calm periods to increase fill rate.

→ OSM: Adverse Selection Monitoring

Given the high fill share (~92%), consider monitoring whether counterparties are consistently better-informed (e.g., tracking mid movement post-fill) and adjusting quote aggressiveness accordingly.
