Section 01

A New Asset Class: From Delta-1 to Options

Round 3 introduced a structurally different problem to the one in Round 1. Whereas Round 1 presented two delta-1 instruments with known regimes — Osmium's mean reversion and Pepper Root's linear trend — Round 3 adds ten European call options (Velvetfruit Extract Vouchers) to two delta-1 assets. This necessitated a fundamental shift in analytical framework.

The key intellectual shift was recognising that options cannot be treated as delta-1 instruments. Their price is not a direct signal of value; it is an encoding of the market's expectation of future volatility. The correct input to any options model is not price — it is Implied Volatility (IV). This is what must be extracted and compared across strikes.

Critical TTE Nuance. The vouchers have a 7-day expiry starting from Round 1 of the competition. During the historical sample data (days 0–2 used for research), TTE decreases: Day 0 → TTE=8d, Day 1 → TTE=7d, Day 2 → TTE=6d. However, for all 10 days of out-of-sample deployment, the TTE is fixed at 5 days — because the algorithm runs during Round 3, when exactly 5 days remain until expiry. This is a critical parameter: using the wrong TTE in Black-Scholes produces systematically biased IV estimates and incorrect delta hedges.

Section 03

Options Research: Implied Volatility & Smile Structure

The options strategy required building a complete analytical pipeline from scratch. The approach followed three stages: (1) compute IV for every strike at every tick using Black-Scholes, (2) map IV against moneyness to identify the structural shape of the smile, (3) locate persistent outliers and design a mean-reverting trade around them.

3.1 — Black-Scholes Engine

A dependency-free Black-Scholes engine was implemented using only Python's math module (required by the competition's sandboxed environment). IV is computed via 40-iteration bisection search, achieving microsecond-level execution per contract. Rate of interest r=0 is assumed throughout — appropriate given the short TTE and the competition's frictionless environment.

Black-Scholes Call Pricing & IV Extraction d₁ = [ln(S/K) + (0 + σ²/2)·T] / (σ·√T)
d₂ = d₁ − σ·√T
C(S,K,T,σ) = S·N(d₁) − K·N(d₂) ← r=0, European call
IV = bisect(σ → C(σ) = market_price, bounds=[0.0001, 5.0], iters=40)

3.2 — Volatility Smile Analysis

Plotting IV against moneyness (S/K) across all three historical days reveals a persistent structural pattern. Across the liquid ATM region (strikes 5000–5500), IV is broadly flat at ~23–24%, consistent with the textbook near-term options smile. However, the VEV_5400 strike (moneyness ≈ 0.97) consistently shows an anomalously low implied volatility — a clear and exploitable dislocation.

3.3 — Moneyness Context

With the underlying trading at ~5,250, VEV_5400 is slightly out-of-the-money (moneyness ≈ 0.97, or ~2.9% OTM). This places it in the most sensitive region of the gamma/vega curve — options at this moneyness have high sensitivity to both underlying moves (delta ~0.35–0.45) and to volatility changes (vega). The structural IV underpricing is therefore both meaningful and exploitable.

Section 04

Options Trading Logic: Structural Volatility Arbitrage

The research identified a robust, persistent structural dislocation in the IV smile. The trading strategy operationalises this into a mean-reverting IV arbitrage position, sized proportionally to the spread magnitude and hedged dynamically for delta exposure.

4.1 — Fair Value Construction

The fair value for VEV_5400's implied volatility is constructed as the midpoint of its immediate neighbours — VEV_5300 and VEV_5500. This is a model-agnostic interpolation: it assumes the smile is locally linear (or at least smooth) around the ATM region, which the historical data confirms. No parametric smile model (SABR, SVI) is required.

IV Fair Value & Spread Signal fair_IV = (IV₅₃₀₀ + IV₅₅₀₀) / 2
spread = fair_IV − IV₅₄₀₀ ← positive = VEV_5400 is cheap → BUY
target_pos₅₄₀₀ = clip(spread × 15,000, −300, +300)
target_pos₅₅₀₀ = −target_pos₅₄₀₀ ← opposing position to fund the trade
Why scale by 15,000? A 1% IV spread (spread = 0.01) maps to a target position of 150 contracts (0.01 × 15,000 = 150). A 2% spread (spread = 0.02) maps to 300 — the full position limit. This is proportional sizing: conviction scales with the magnitude of the dislocation, exactly as the options market theory dictates. It is the same principle applied in Round 1's t-statistic gate, now applied to a volatility signal rather than a price signal.

4.2 — Delta Hedging

A pure options position carries unwanted directional exposure to the underlying. To isolate the volatility signal — and only capture the IV mean-reversion — the net delta of the options book is continuously hedged using Velvetfruit Extract. This transforms the trade from a directional bet into a pure volatility position.

The Extract hedge target is updated every tick. When VEV_5400 is bought (positive position), its delta (~0.35–0.45) is partially offset by the short VEV_5500 position, with any residual hedged by selling Extract. This ensures the strategy's PnL is driven by IV convergence, not by directional moves in the underlying.

4.3 — The TTE Parameter: A Critical Input

In the backtest engine, TTE is computed dynamically from the timestamp: T = max(0.0001, (5.0 − timestamp / 1,000,000) / 365.0). This correctly models that TTE decays from 5 days to ~4 days over the course of a single OOS trading day. Both the IV calculation and the delta calculation are sensitive to T — a wrong TTE produces wrong deltas and therefore incorrect hedge ratios.

Practical implication: During the historical sample (days 0–2), TTE is 8d, 7d, and 6d respectively. Had these been used in the OOS backtest, every IV and delta calculation would be biased. The algorithm correctly uses T = (5.0 − ts/1M) / 365 for OOS deployment, reflecting the true 5-day expiry of the live round.

Section 06

Post-Mortem & Forward Improvements

Assessment

Round 3 represents a significant increase in analytical complexity relative to Round 1. The core insight — that VEV_5400 is structurally mispriced in IV space — was identified through rigorous exploratory data analysis across all three historical days, and held consistently in the data. The Black-Scholes engine, implemented without any external dependencies, delivered robust IV estimates, and the proportional sizing mechanism correctly concentrated exposure where the edge was widest.

The most important methodological advance was the correct handling of TTE: recognising that the OOS TTE is fixed at 5 days — not the historical sample TTEs — and building this directly into the pricing and hedging logic. This is the kind of structural nuance that separates systematic approaches from naïve implementations.

✗ No Broader Strike Coverage

Only VEV_5400/5500 were traded. VEV_5300 was used only as a reference for fair IV, not as a trading vehicle. Extending the arbitrage across more legs of the smile could capture additional IV dislocations (e.g., VEV_6000 showed elevated IV at ~35–40%).

✗ Static Fair-Value Model

Fair IV is interpolated linearly from two neighbouring strikes. A quadratic (or parametric) smile fit would be more robust — especially as the underlying moves and moneyness shifts, causing the nearest-neighbour model to break down.

⚠ Discrete Delta Hedging

Delta is recomputed and hedged on every tick, but integer constraints on the Extract position mean the hedge is never exact. Gamma exposure (convexity in delta) accumulates intraday and is not separately managed.

⚠ Session-End Risk

At expiry, open voucher positions are liquidated against a hidden fair value. If the IV dislocation has not fully closed, there is residual theta and vega risk at session end. An EOD position unwinding protocol (similar to the delta-1 circuit breaker) would be valuable.
